<div class='post-header-line-1'></div>
</div>
<div class='post-body entry-content' id='post-body-3387110411627413880' itemprop='description articleBody'>

So it didn't get passed off.&nbsp;<div>It reminded me of Coach Bell's shop class many decades ago. We all had woodworking projects and we were required to stain them in order to get a grade. Some kids went to ol' Coach and tried to convince him to let them leave their cigar boxes/book ends natural...Coach asked them," What do you call that when you don't put stain on it?"</div><div>"Unfinished."</div><div>"There you go, stain it."</div>

<h3 class='post-title entry-title' itemprop='name'>
<a href='http://rmsbandbasicskills.blogspot.com/2014/05/clarity-in-rehearsal.html'>Clarity in Rehearsal</a>
</h3>
<div class='post-header'>
<div class='post-header-line-1'></div>
</div>
<div class='post-body entry-content' id='post-body-7762302110247276618' itemprop='description articleBody'>
Clarity in Rehearsal Regardless of the difficulty level of the music...Or the age of the musicians you're teaching. <break>There are always moments in the early stages of executing your sight-reading procedure that can be likened to looking into a murky pond. Balance, pitch, rhythms, dynamics, articulations, tempo. All these things can be tweaked, tugged, shaped and managed until all of a sudden the picture slowly becomes more clear. <break>The imagery I like to think of is the old Oxy-Clean commercial where a scoop of the detergent is poured into a murky bucket and in seconds the water becomes transparent. That's what we should be working for, as teachers and students, to bring clarity to the composer's intentions. <break>One of my favorite Jim Smith-isms is: 
<blockquote>
"Trumpets, that sounds as if you're trying to push a banana through a screen door.......It can be done......But there's not a lot of clarity on the other side...."</blockquote>
</break></break></break>

<blockquote>Another issue in clarity is a committment to over-doing articulation. The great Roger Bobo draws parallels between how a musician articulates a note with how an artist uses a specific paintbrush to make a certain kind of line. It brings clarity of thought, of ideas to your audience. Think of it this way: If you were trying to impress the object of your affections, would you write them a note in beautiful caligraphy or finger paints?</blockquote>

<h3 class='post-title entry-title' itemprop='name'>
<a href='http://rmsbandbasicskills.blogspot.com/2014/01/long-tones-parts-2-and-25.html'>LONG TONES, Parts 2 and 2.5</a>
</h3>
<div class='post-header'>
<div class='post-header-line-1'></div>
</div>
<div class='post-body entry-content' id='post-body-8956492803570006900' itemprop='description articleBody'>
<div>
<div class="separator" style="clear: both;">
</div>
<div class="separator" style="clear: both;"><div class="separator" style="clear: both;"><br></div><div class="separator" style="clear: both;"><a href="https://lh3.googleusercontent.com/-qNfythhysXc/Ut5ybAYgECI/AAAAAAAAAYI/v_OeykfH64M/s640/blogger-image--1189545728.jpg" imageanchor="1" style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;"><img border="0" src="https://lh3.googleusercontent.com/-qNfythhysXc/Ut5ybAYgECI/AAAAAAAAAYI/v_OeykfH64M/s640/blogger-image--1189545728.jpg"></a></div><div class="separator" style="clear: both;"><br></div><br></div>
<br></div>
<div>
<br></div>
So, long tones are pretty boring in themselves, but there's so much you can do to them to engage student learning!!! In Part 1, we learned how to teach different registers how to sound the same, how to control to our sound at different dynamic levels and how to sub-divide like a FOOL - all while playing with proper balance and blend!<br>
<br>
Well, in parts 2 and 2.5, we take that knowledge and expand it somewhat. We take the boring old musical gruel of the CHROMATIC SCALE and we fluff it up a bit, (give it a disco make-over like is so popular with all the young kids today) incorporating dynamic changes, double top-secret clarinet fingerings and some pretty slammin' drum rudiments.<br>
<br>
In Long Tone 2.5, we incorporate all the fun we used in long tones 1 and 2 and we have an honest to goodness band etude in unison, except for the drums, of course, who are still grooving in rudiment city.<br>
<br>
So- Download these, try them with your band. As long as your students are making a great tone, counting like crazy, reinforcing good habits, and having fun-There is no end to the benefit of these exercises!<br>

<br />
From beginners to seasoned professionals, LONG TONES are always important to developing a great sound and solid technique on an instrument-It's the musician's equivalent of lifting weights or PUMPING IRON- But with YO FACE!!!! (OR YO STICKS!!!!)<br />
<div>
While LONG TONES can be tedious, they are some of the most important things a musician can play as a part of their daily routine.
