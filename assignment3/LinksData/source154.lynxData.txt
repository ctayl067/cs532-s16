[3]How good is good enough?

   Four first-year high school (freshmen) flute students wanted to pass
   off their halftime show memorization last week. They can play the show
   but it was horribly out of tune. So it didn't get passed off.
   It reminded me of Coach Bell's shop class many decades ago. We all had
   woodworking projects and we were required to stain them in order to get
   a grade. Some kids went to ol' Coach and tried to convince him to let
   them leave their cigar boxes/book ends natural...Coach asked them,"
   What do you call that when you don't put stain on it?"
   "Unfinished."
   "There you go, stain it."

[66]Clarity in Rehearsal

   Clarity in Rehearsal Regardless of the difficulty level of the
   music...Or the age of the musicians you're teaching. There are always
   moments in the early stages of executing your sight-reading procedure
   that can be likened to looking into a murky pond. Balance, pitch,
   rhythms, dynamics, articulations, tempo. All these things can be
   tweaked, tugged, shaped and managed until all of a sudden the picture
   slowly becomes more clear. The imagery I like to think of is the old
   Oxy-Clean commercial where a scoop of the detergent is poured into a
   murky bucket and in seconds the water becomes transparent. That's what
   we should be working for, as teachers and students, to bring clarity to
   the composer's intentions. One of my favorite Jim Smith-isms is:

     "Trumpets, that sounds as if you're trying to push a banana through
     a screen door.......It can be done......But there's not a lot of
     clarity on the other side...."

     Another issue in clarity is a committment to over-doing
     articulation. The great Roger Bobo draws parallels between how a
     musician articulates a note with how an artist uses a specific
     paintbrush to make a certain kind of line. It brings clarity of
     thought, of ideas to your audience. Think of it this way: If you
     were trying to impress the object of your affections, would you
     write them a note in beautiful caligraphy or finger paints?

[87]LONG TONES, Parts 2 and 2.5

   [88][blogger-image--1189545728.jpg]
   So, long tones are pretty boring in themselves, but there's so much you
   can do to them to engage student learning!!! In Part 1, we learned how
   to teach different registers how to sound the same, how to control to
   our sound at different dynamic levels and how to sub-divide like a FOOL
   - all while playing with proper balance and blend!
   Well, in parts 2 and 2.5, we take that knowledge and expand it
   somewhat. We take the boring old musical gruel of the CHROMATIC SCALE
   and we fluff it up a bit, (give it a disco make-over like is so popular
   with all the young kids today) incorporating dynamic changes, double
   top-secret clarinet fingerings and some pretty slammin' drum rudiments.
   In Long Tone 2.5, we incorporate all the fun we used in long tones 1
   and 2 and we have an honest to goodness band etude in unison, except
   for the drums, of course, who are still grooving in rudiment city.
   So- Download these, try them with your band. As long as your students
   are making a great tone, counting like crazy, reinforcing good habits,
   and having fun-There is no end to the benefit of these exercises!

[123]Basic Skill Number One: LONG TONES, Part 1

   [124][dog-funny-face.jpg]
   (This kid hasn't been playing his long tones!)
   From beginners to seasoned professionals, LONG TONES are always
   important to developing a great sound and solid technique on an
   instrument-It's the musician's equivalent of lifting weights or PUMPING
   IRON- But with YO FACE!!!! (OR YO STICKS!!!!)

   things a musician can play as a part of their daily routine. Try
