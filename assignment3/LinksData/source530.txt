<h3 class='post-title entry-title' itemprop='name'>
<a href='http://iraq-solidarity.blogspot.com/2016/02/isil-losing-appeal-in-iraq-but-threat.html'>ISIL &#8216;losing appeal&#8217; in Iraq, but threat should not be underestimated &#8211; UN envoy</a>
</h3>
<div class='post-header'>
<div class='post-header-line-1'></div>
</div>
<div class='post-body entry-content' id='post-body-3188680712844330897' itemprop='articleBody'>
<div style="text-align: justify;">
Iraq has been steadily gaining ground against the Islamic State of Iraq and the Levant (ISIL, also known as Da&#8217;esh), which is gradually losing its appeal to the disenfranchised population, the United Nations envoy for that country told the Security Council, as he also warned that its threat should not be underestimated.&nbsp;</div>
<div style="text-align: justify;">
<br /></div>
<div style="text-align: justify;">
<a href="https://4.bp.blogspot.com/-t6510QhmkMU/VsRDLfsmteI/AAAAAAAAE14/NlMEbYcJ_2s/s1600/iraqi%2Bchild.jpg" imageanchor="1" style="clear: right; float: right; margin-bottom: 1em; margin-left: 1em;"><img border="0" height="212" src="https://4.bp.blogspot.com/-t6510QhmkMU/VsRDLfsmteI/AAAAAAAAE14/NlMEbYcJ_2s/s320/iraqi%2Bchild.jpg" width="320" /></a>Briefing the 15-nation body, the Special Representative of the Secretary-General for Iraq, Jan Kubiš, said the liberation and holding of Beiji, Sinjar, and most of all Ramadi, and continuing clearance of the surrounding areas from ISIL fighters, have instilled the people of Iraq with hope that the country can and will be liberated from ISIL.&nbsp;</div>
<div style="text-align: justify;">
<br /></div>
<div style="text-align: justify;">
The success also demonstrates the increasingly resolute and effective support to Iraq of the global coalition to counter ISIL and provides lessons for preparations to liberate the remaining territories, most notably of Mosul, he said.&nbsp;</div>
<div style="text-align: justify;">
<br /></div>
<div style="text-align: justify;">
Turning to the issue of Turkish forces present in Camp Bashiqa, mutually acceptable results have not been achieved so far, he said, reiterating the calls made by the Secretary-General for a solution in full respect of the sovereignty and territorial integrity of Iraq &#8211; the principles set forth in the UN Charter.&nbsp;
</div>
<div style="text-align: justify;">
<br /></div>
<div style="text-align: justify;">
Pointing to an absence of political consensus, Mr. Kubiš said the stability, security and unity of Iraq hinge on an effective and inclusive political system and equality in decision-making at the federal and local levels. Tangible solutions must include amendments to or adoption of priority legislation, followed by swift implementation, such as the Accountability and Justice Law, the National Guard Law and the General Amnesty Law.&nbsp;</div>
<div style="text-align: justify;">
<br /></div>
<div style="text-align: justify;">
Full and equal participation of the Sunni component in, and its co-ownership of, the national reconciliation programme is still a challenge, he said. The absence of a single framework or vision for national reconciliation in Iraq is both symptomatic of long-existing problems and further impedes efforts to advance inclusive national and community dialogue and historic compromise.&nbsp;</div>
<div style="text-align: justify;">
<br /></div>
<div style="text-align: justify;">
Efforts by Sunni political forces and their leaders to unify their stance on national reconciliation, effective federalisation and decentralisation, and how to more successfully counter ISIL are equally necessary, he added.

The UN Assistance Mission for Iraq (UNAMI), which Mr. Kubiš heads, organized a series of events earlier this month to mark World Interfaith Harmony Week in Najaf, Baghdad and Erbil.&nbsp;</div>
<div style="text-align: justify;">
<br /></div>
<div style="text-align: justify;">
The events promoted dialogue on preventative steps to address drivers of violent extremism, in consonance with the United Nations Plan of Action to Prevent Violent Extremism.

Turning to the safe return of the internally displaced persons (IDPs), he emphasized that the stabilization phase of Tikrit is almost complete, with 90 per cent of its population, or 167,000 people, have now returned home.&nbsp;</div>
<div style="text-align: justify;">
<br /></div>
<div style="text-align: justify;">
So far, more than 500,000 displaced Iraqis have returned to their home communities. The Government intends that up to 900,000 will return in 2016, despite several complicated factors, such as a huge number of improvised explosive devices (IEDs) laid by ISIL, as well as devastating destruction to infrastructure and homes.&nbsp;</div>
<div style="text-align: justify;">
<br /></div>
<div style="text-align: justify;">
Iraq&#8217;s persistent and rapidly-deepening fiscal crisis and growing budget deficit, compounded by the security and humanitarian situation and drastic decline in global oil prices, has almost halved the State&#8217;s planned income since then, and the Kurdistan Region faces at least a situation as grave as that of Baghdad, he noted.&nbsp;</div>
<div style="text-align: justify;">
<br /></div>
<div style="text-align: justify;">
If left unaddressed, such an unsustainable situation may seriously undermine the renewed morale of pro-government forces and confidence of the people, including youths, communities, minorities and IDPs that they can have a future in Iraq, he added.&nbsp;</div>
<div style="text-align: justify;">
<br /></div>
<div style="text-align: justify;">
On the human rights front, Mr. Kubiš supported the calls of the Human Rights Council, the High Commissioner for Human Rights, and the Special Advisor to the Secretary-General on the Prevention of Genocide, that Iraq should consider becoming a Party to the Rome Statute of the International Criminal Court and to the Additional Protocol II to the Geneva Conventions 1949, and that the Iraqi criminal code be amended to grant Iraqi courts the jurisdiction to deal with international crimes.&nbsp;</div>
<div style="text-align: justify;">
<br /></div>
<div style="text-align: justify;">
The humanitarian crisis in Iraq is highly complex, and is expected to widen and worsen this year. Today not only 3.3 million IDPs, but altogether some 10 million Iraqis &#8211; almost one third of the population &#8211; urgently require some form of humanitarian assistance, he said.&nbsp;</div>
<div style="text-align: justify;">
<br /></div>
<div style="text-align: justify;">
Concluding his remarks, he informed the Security Council of the news that Amer al-Kaissy, UNAMI's liaison officer abducted in April 2015 in Diyala, was found dead, strongly urging the Iraqi authorities to conduct immediately a thorough and transparent investigation into this abduction and murder and hold the perpetrators accountable.&nbsp;</div>
<div style="text-align: justify;">
<br /></div>
<div style="text-align: justify;">
In a<b><i> <a href="http://www.un.org/sg/statements/index.asp?nid=9470" target="_blank">statement</a></i></b>, the Secretary-General condemned the &#8220;shocking murder,&#8221; verified on 15 February, and expressed his condolences to his family and friends.
</div>

<h3 class='post-title entry-title' itemprop='name'>
<a href='http://iraq-solidarity.blogspot.com/2016/02/after-25-years-of-us-role-in-iraq-scars.html'>After 25 Years of U.S. Role in Iraq, Scars Are Too Stubborn to Fade</a>
</h3>
<div class='post-header'>
<div class='post-header-line-1'></div>
</div>
<div class='post-body entry-content' id='post-body-8678041611171369418' itemprop='articleBody'>
<div style="text-align: justify;">
It was once a well-kept memorial for hundreds of Iraqi civilians who were killed by American bombs while they slept, a powerful symbol of suffering embraced by a dictator&#8217;s propaganda machine.

The destruction of the Amiriya bomb shelter, in a middle-class Baghdad neighborhood, on Feb. 13, 1991, at the outset of the Persian Gulf war, killed some 408 civilians in the worst way possible: Most were burned alive.&#160;</div>
<div style="text-align: justify;">
<br></div>
<div style="text-align: justify;">
It stands as the deadliest episode of civilian casualties in the painful history, now a quarter-century long, of the United States in Iraq.

For years, Saddam Hussein ensured that the event remained etched in the Iraqi collective memory, recalled in movies, songs, poems and ceremonies. The shelter was a required stop for visiting dignitaries and foreign correspondents in Mr. Hussein&#8217;s tightly controlled Iraq.&#160;</div>
<div style="text-align: justify;">
<br></div>
<div style="text-align: justify;">
<a href="https://1.bp.blogspot.com/-XUTjuC5VPIY/VsRIjCLrrcI/AAAAAAAAE2g/LwSxgRQbI3Q/s1600/amiriya%2Bmassacre.jpg" imageanchor="1" style="clear: right; float: right; margin-bottom: 1em; margin-left: 1em;"><img border="0" height="222" src="https://1.bp.blogspot.com/-XUTjuC5VPIY/VsRIjCLrrcI/AAAAAAAAE2g/LwSxgRQbI3Q/s320/amiriya%2Bmassacre.jpg" width="320"></a>This week, 25 years later, the anniversary passed with almost no notice. An Iraqi Army unit now occupies the grounds of the shelter. The site is no longer open to the public, although the occasional survivors or family members of the dead are welcomed by soldiers and given tours.

A few days ago, on the anniversary, two men from the neighborhood, now in their 60s, greeted a reporter and spoke about their memories.&#160;</div>
<div style="text-align: justify;">
<br></div>
<div style="text-align: justify;">
Each had lost several family members in the attack, and each had helped pile the charred bodies on trucks.

One of the men, Hussein Abdella, 63, a retired truck driver, pointed to the neglected gravestones, chipped and overgrown with weeds and wildflowers, and said: &#8220;These are just names. There are no bodies buried here. They were all burnt. You could not recognize the bodies.&#8221;&#160;</div>
<div style="text-align: justify;">
<br></div>
<div style="text-align: justify;">
Shorn of its political purpose, the building itself is now a sad and neglected time capsule. The structure remains just as the American bunker-busting bombs left it, a scar on a cityscape that remains disfigured, too, by strikes from the opening of the 2003 war. There is a giant hole in the roof, a giant crater in the earth and a gnarled mess of wires and protruding steel rods.&#160;</div>
<div style="text-align: justify;">
<br></div>
<div style="text-align: justify;">
In dark corners down below are the dusty remnants of what the place once meant &#8212; and still does to some degree &#8212; to the Iraqi nation: black-and-white photos of the victims, arranged by family, fastened to poster boards but no longer hanging from the walls.

Many of those pictured are small children: Hussein Alaa Ibrahim and his sister, Samah; Faras Khadim Abbas and his brother Amjid; Karrar Talib and his sister Hadeel.&#160;</div>
<div style="text-align: justify;">
<br></div>
<div style="text-align: justify;">
There is a tattered poster from the second anniversary of the bombing, bearing Mr. Hussein&#8217;s face and the words, &#8220;Allegiance and Challenge: The Second Anniversary of the Immortal Mother of Battles.&#8221;&#160;</div>
<div style="text-align: justify;">
<br></div>
<div style="text-align: justify;">
For the two neighborhood men, the anniversary of the shelter bombing was an occasion for reflection on a stark milestone: The gulf war began in January 1991, and so by now the United States has been using its military to shape events in Iraq for more than a quarter-century. And there is no end in sight: The next American president will almost surely be the fifth in succession to order airstrikes in Iraq.<br>
<br></div>
<div style="text-align: justify;">
